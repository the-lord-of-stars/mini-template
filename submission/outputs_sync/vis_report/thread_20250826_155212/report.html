    1. Quantitative Trend: How much (and when) was automated visualization researched?

    I labeled AutoVis papers using a repeatable text-based filter (matching keywords such as “autovis”, “automated”, “autonom”, and related stems across Title, Abstract, and AuthorKeywords) and then tracked counts and proportions over time and by venue. The filtered set spans 1995–2024 and in this assembled dataset every record meets the AutoVis criteria, so the series represents the AutoVis subset itself rather than a tiny fraction of all visualization output. Over the period there is clear episodic growth rather than steady linear change: modest early activity in the 2000s, a notable jump in 2014, a much larger surge across 2020–2021 (2021 is the single busiest year with 16 AutoVis papers), a drop in 2022, and then another rebound in 2023. Several years stand out for impact as well as volume — 2013, 2015 and 2018 host the most-cited papers — and many years (2006–2010 and a long run from 2013–2021) show above-median citation or download signals, indicating that spikes in production often align with genuine attention. The work is strongly concentrated in core Vis venues: the Vis umbrella (45 papers), VAST (21) and InfoVis (13) account for the majority of AutoVis publications, underlining that automated visualization research remains centered in the main visualization conferences.

    3. What topics and methods dominated — and where did the field go next?

    The thematic analysis groups AutoVis work into a small set of recurrent themes — Recommendation, Interaction/Interface, Generation, Mixed-Initiative, and Agent/Autonomous — and traces how those themes evolve and map to impact. Recommendation is the largest theme by paper count (23 papers) and has strong download numbers; Generation papers are fewer (15) but have the highest mean citations (≈45), indicating outsized scholarly influence per paper. Temporal traces show that Generation appeared as early as 1995, Recommendation and Interaction became more visible in the 2010s, and Recommendation surges again after 2020 (notably 2021). The three top-cited AutoVis publications (Voyager 2015, Draco 2018, and the 2013 design-space paper) anchor major lines of subsequent work: recommendation and automated design frameworks drove both subsequent research and uptake. Despite these advances, gaps remain — rigorous, reproducible evaluation and real-world deployment studies are underrepresented, and where GraphicsReplicabilityStamp or similar markers exist they are rarely common; this suggests that the field’s next step should emphasize evaluation standards, reproducibility, benchmarks, and longitudinal or in-the-wild studies that connect methods to real user benefit.

The time series of paper counts by theme reveals when each approach rose: Generation has early roots (1995) and resurfaces strongly in the late 2010s; Recommendation intensifies in 2020–2021. Linking themes to impact suggests that system- and framework-oriented work (e.g., Voyager, Draco) has been particularly influential, whereas evaluation-focused or real-world deployment studies are less common. The visualization supports a conclusion for the community: invest more in reproducible evaluations, shared benchmarks, and deployments to convert methodological innovation into sustained, verifiable impact.
